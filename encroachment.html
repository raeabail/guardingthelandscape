Encroachment Through Incompatible Land Use

When Growth Meets the Ground

Camp Atterbury’s protected interior has remained largely intact since 1942, but the landscape at its edges tells a different story. Over eight decades, rural farmland and forests have gradually transformed into a suburban frontier, where homes, roads, and industrial corridors now press against training lands and wildlife habitat.


This section combines two lenses, encroachment and incompatible land use, to trace how changing development patterns are reshaping the region, and what those trends mean for military readiness, ecological integrity, and community resilience.

Encroachment refers to the gradual expansion of residential and commercial growth toward military installations. Around Camp Atterbury, this takes the form of surburban sprawl along the Greenwood-Franklin-Edinburgh corridor, annexations near Columbis, and amenity-driven rural housing in Brown County's forests.


Encroachment itself is not inherently negative, but when new development intersects sensitive areas such as bat habitat, floodplains, and recharge zones, it becomes an incompatible land use challenge.

Incompatible land use occurs when development patterns or infrastructure undermine training capability, enviornmental quality, or public safety. This includes new homes or schools near live-fire or flight zones, as well as residential and commericial development into floodplains or areas known to support sensitive species such as bat habitat.


Importantly, new septic systems are not themselves a threat. They represen regulated development activity. However, the density and placement of new septic permits provide a strong indicator of where development and forest fragmentation are occurring, especially in rural, unsewered areas like Brown County.

1940s-1960s: Establishment and Early Decades

  • Camp Atterbury was established in 1942 on roughly 40,000 acres spanning Johnson, Bartholomew, and Brown Counties.
  • Dozens of farmsteads, small communities, and schools were displaced, transforming a rural agricultural mosaic into a structured military training landscape.
  • The surrounding region remained dominated by cropland and forest, with limited population density and minimal land subdivision.
  • Incompatible land use was virtually nonexistent. Rural livelihoods, forest buffers, and training operations coexisted with little interference, as illustrated in the swipe map's aerial view.
  • Brown County's heavily forested terrain served as a natural buffer between the installation and surrounding towns.

1970s-1990s: Suburban Creep Southward

  • As Indianapolis expanded south, early suburban corridors formed along US-31 and I-65 in Johnson and Bartholomew Counties, replacing farmland with residential subdivisions.
  • Farmland fragmentation accelerated near Franklin, Whiteland, and Edinburgh. These linear corridors would later become long-term encroachment vectors.
  • Brown County remained over 90% forested, but private lake communities such as Prince’s Lakes and Cordry–Sweetwater introduced amenity-driven rural development. As shown in the 1980–today land-use swipe map (and further detailed in the Cordry–Sweetwater Case Study), these early clusters marked significant breaks in the county’s continuous forest cover.
  • Septic permits began increasing in unsewered areas, marking early signs of forest fragmentation. While compliant, their clustering indicated growth pressure in sensitive terrain.
  • Atterbury's perimeter buffers were still largely intact, but future encroachment patterns were taking shape along transportation corridors.

2000s-2010s: A Landscape Transformed

  • Residential and industrial expansion intensified along I-65 and US-31, permanently converting productive farmland to suburban and commercial corridors.
  • Impervious surface increased by nearly 50% from 2001 to 2010, reshaping stormwater runoff and degrading habitat connectivity north of Camp Atterbury.
  • Subdivisions increasingly overlapped mapped bat habitat, floodplains, and groundwater recharge areas, signaling the onset of widespread incompatible land use.
  • Bartholomew County's west-side annexations near SR-46 expanded development toward riparian corridors once buffered by farmland.

2010s-2020s: The Current Pulse of Conversion

  • Farmland conversion reached a new level of permanence. Indiana lost an estimated 1.5-2% of agricultural land between 2010 and 2022 due to industrial, residential, and commercial development.
  • Johnson County remains a conversion hotspot where suburban and industrial zoning has replaced much of the former agricultural buffers north of Camp Atterbury.
  • Septic and building permits in Brown County reveal steady forest subdivision, marking diffuse but ecologically significant fragmentation.
  • Development along SR-46 and US-31 increasingly overlaps flood-fringe zones and wetland buffers, raising long-term flood management costs and ecological risk. The SR-46 Chase Point rezoning proposal case study exemplifies this trend and is highlighted in the swipe map.

2040 & Beyond: Turning the Tide or Paying the Price

  • Farms Under Threat 2040 projects continued sprawl along the Greenwood, Franklin, and Edinburgh corridor unless proactive zoning and conservation investments occur.
  • If unmanaged, residential and industrial growth will further reduce compatible buffers, fragment bat and bird habitat, and encroach on Atterbury’s night-training zones.
  • Land-use modeling shows that targeted conservation easements, compatible-use zoning overlays, and rural growth boundaries could retain over 20 percent more open space by 2050 compared to business-as-usual trends.
  • The path forward depends on integrating the Southern Indiana Sentinel Landscape partnership, REPI conservation tools, and other agricultural land protections that promote clustered development, groundwater protection, and forest connectivity.

Encroachment Risk

Development pressure around Camp Atterbury’s perimeter does not occur evenly. The north and northeast experience the strongest encroachment risk driven by suburban growth. The east and southeast show moderate expansion linked to corridor-based development, while the west and southwest remain comparatively stable due to steep, forested terrain that slows residential subdivision activity.

Growth in Numbers, Patterns of Change

The EVA findings help illustrate where development pressure is occurring, but long-term demographic and land-use trends explain why those pressures have emerged. Over the last several decades, population growth, septic density increases, and land conversion have steadily reshaped the region surrounding Camp Atterbury.


As the counties north and northeast of the installation urbanized, development expanded south from Indianapolis in Johnson County, outward from Columbus in Bartholomew County, and into the amenity-driven uplands of Brown County. These trajectories mirror the EVA development patterns shown above and reveal how encroachment pressure has gradually intensified along specific borders of the installation.


In rural, unsewered areas, especially Brown County, clusters of new septic permits provide some of the earliest signals of forest fragmentation and low-density subdivision growth. These changes often appear years before satellite-based land-cover updates, making septic trends a valuable early indicator of emerging encroachment corridors.

Septic permit density over time reveals early development patterns, especially in rural uplands where subdivision expansion precedes noticeable land-cover change.


Taken together, these long-term growth and development trajectories and early indicators (such as septic permit clustering) form a clear picture of how development pressure has expanded toward Camp Atterbury, narrowing compatible-use buffers over time. But understanding why these patterns matter requires examining their direct implications for training and ecological resilience.

Training Readiness and Habitat Integrity

As development moves closer to Camp Atterbury, its operational isolation diminishes. Its effects are felt not only on the landscape but also in the day-to-day function of Camp Atterbury’s training environment and the surrounding ecological systems. These impacts fall into two interconnected dimensions: training readiness and habitat integrity.

Training Readiness
  • Encroaching residential and commercial development increases noise and light conflicts, leading to more community complaints and operational restrictions.
  • Reduced night-training and range flexibility as safety buffers expand and incompatible land uses—such as industrial, residential, and commercial development—move closer to training areas.
  • Loss of rural buffer and natural terrain fragments maneuver corridors, limits large-scale exercises, and erodes the isolation needed for realistic training.
Habitat Integrity

Farmland conversion and suburban expansion around the installation disrupt the surrounding ecosystem, creating ecological pressures that include:

  • Conversion of farmland and woodlots erodes natural buffers around the installation and increases habitat isolation.
  • Disrupted forest connectivity limits movement and foraging habitat for bats, migratory birds, and other wildlife species.
  • Expanding development edges intensify light, noise, and invasive-species pressures along the installation boundary.

Conflict Hotspots — Where Growth Meets Sensitivity

When these readiness and habitat considerations are viewed alongside EVA development patterns and long-term demographic trends, a set of clear conflict zones emerges. These are the areas where rapidly changing land use directly intersects with sensitive ecological features or mission-critical training buffers around Camp Atterbury. Planning for Balance

Encroachment and incompatible land use do not have to be inevitable. They are the cumulative result of local and regional planning decisions. By aligning zoning, conservation, and infrastructure with compatible-use principles, Indiana can protect both rural character and national defense readiness.

A balanced approach requires coordinating zoning, conservation investments, and infrastructure planning so that community development and national defense can coexist without conflict.

Key Actions

  • Expand compatible-use zoning overlays in high-risk corridors.
  • Prioritize conservation easements in undeveloped buffer areas around Atterbury.
  • Incentivize cluster development and open-space preservation.
  • Support REPI and Sentinel Landscape partnerships that integrate defense, agriculture, and ecology.

Looking Toward 2050

Camp Atterbury’s landscape will continue evolving. The question is not whether growth will occur, but how well it will coexist with the missions and habitats that define this place. Through data-driven mapping, historic land records, and forward-thinking policy, Indiana can build a future where growth and stewardship align rather than collide.

Chase Point Development – SR-46 Corridor

Location: Northeast corner of SR-46 & County Road 500 W in West Columbus, Bartholomew County.


Proposal: The city is considering annexing and rezoning about 107 acres of farmland at SR-46 and County Road 500 W from Agriculture: Preferred (AP) to Residential (RS-4 and RM). The proposed “Chase Point” project would include about 250 single-family homes and a multi-family section planned for later phases, transforming what is currently working farmland and wooded open space into suburban residential development.


Why it matters:

  • Converts productive agricultural land and wooded open space along a flood-prone corridor into suburban housing.
  • Replaces one of the last farmland buffers between Columbus’s west side and the Tipton Lakes area, pushing the suburban edge northward toward Camp Atterbury.
  • Reflects Indiana’s broader trend of farmland-to-housing conversion, requiring new infrastructure and traffic improvements along SR-46 and CR-500 W.
  • Introduces development within mapped floodplain and flood-fringe zones, increasing future flood-management costs and ecological disturbance.
  • Embodies the “corridor-edge farmland” conversion pattern identified by AFT’s Farms Under Threat 2040, where annexations fill in formerly agricultural transition zones.

Cordry–Sweetwater Lakes – A Manufactured Shoreline

1 / 5

From Forested Valleys to Lakefront Living

While encroachment today often conjures images of suburban sprawl and highway corridors, the forces reshaping Indiana’s landscapes began long before modern suburbs. The creation of Cordry and Sweetwater Lakes offers an early example of how land conversion, much like the trends revealed in our land cover and population data, can permanently redefine buffers, ownership, and ecological connectivity around key areas of the state.

A Tale of Development, Debt, and a Drawn Fenceline

The Cordry–Sweetwater Lakes development began in the 1950s during a boom in private lake construction across southern Indiana. Developer Howard Prince, known for creating Prince’s Lakes, envisioned twin reservoirs joined by a canal to maximize lakefront property. But financial troubles led to bankruptcy, receivership, and the formation of a new governing body.

The Conservancy Takes Shape

In 1961, the Cordry–Sweetwater Conservancy District (CSCD) was established by court order to complete the dams and manage utilities for the lakeside community. Both dams were finished by the late 1960s, Sweetwater in 1967 and Cordry in 1968, though the connecting canal was never built due to rising costs.

The “Fenceline” and Private Ownership

CSCD owns all roads, parks, and the lakes themselves, which are reserved for lot owners and their guests. Property lines stop at the “fenceline”, marking the boundary between private land and conservancy-owned lakebed—once dry ground that became submerged when the dams filled. Lot owners have lake privileges, but not lake ownership.

Relevance to Encroachment and Compatibility

The Cordry–Sweetwater Lakes represent an early form of encroachment, rooted not in urban expansion but in lifestyle-driven transformation. By converting forested valleys into private, recreational property, the development blurred the line between natural and built environments.


Its legacy underscores how cultural values, land speculation, and evolving ownership models have long shaped Indiana’s landscapes. This transformation foreshadowed the development pressures now visible around Camp Atterbury, where property demand and limited planning coordination continue to drive fragmentation.


Cordry–Sweetwater’s story is both a historical lesson and a cautionary example: even well-intentioned projects can alter watershed function, access, and habitat connectivity for generations.
